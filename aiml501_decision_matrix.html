Pre-Trained Model Decision Matrix

Comparing five frontier pre-trained models across NLP, Computer Vision, and Tabular domains on size, accuracy, speed, and explainability
Derrick Karake · AIML-501 Workshop 5 · Portfolio Artifact

Why This Matters

Choosing a pre-trained model is rarely about picking "the best" — it is about picking the right trade-off for a specific deployment. A model that wins on benchmarks may be too large to serve at scale, too slow for an edge device, or too opaque for a regulated industry. This decision matrix compares five widely used pre-trained models across the dimensions that actually drive deployment decisions: model size, accuracy, inference speed, explainability, and ease of integration. The recommendation that follows depends entirely on what you are trying to ship.

Recommendations by Scenario

"I need to ship a chatbot that reasons well"

Use GPT-4 or Claude via API. Accept the latency and cost. Add citation grounding to recover some explainability and run prompt-injection filtering in your application layer.

"I need fast NLP classification at scale"

Fine-tune BERT-base or DistilBERT. You get 90% of the accuracy of a frontier model at 1% of the inference cost, and the model is small enough to serve on commodity hardware.

"I need image recognition on a phone"

MobileNetV3. Designed specifically for edge inference; ships well in TFLite and Core ML. EfficientNet is overkill unless you have a server backing the call.

"I need image classification with the best accuracy"

EfficientNet-B7 or similar. Acknowledge the latency cost and plan your serving stack accordingly. Use Grad-CAM for explainability on a per-prediction basis.

"I have tabular data and need to defend every decision"

XGBoost with SHAP. Tabular data is where classical ML still beats deep learning on most production benchmarks, and SHAP gives you a defensible per-prediction explanation that regulated industries accept.

"I'm working in a regulated industry"

Prioritize explainability over raw accuracy. XGBoost for tabular; BERT with attention visualization for text classification; avoid frontier LLMs for binding decisions unless you have a strong reviewer in the loop.

Conclusion

There is no universally best pre-trained model. There is only the right model for a specific combination of accuracy target, latency budget, deployment footprint, explainability requirement, and team expertise. The discipline of model selection is to be honest about which of those constraints actually binds your project — and to pick the simplest model that meets all of them. A 110M parameter BERT that ships next month will deliver more value than a 175B parameter GPT-4 deployment that never makes it past compliance review.

Explainability is the dimension most often under-weighted in academic comparisons and most often decisive in production. In regulated domains, the model that can explain itself wins even if it loses a few accuracy points. That is the trade-off this matrix is meant to make visible.
