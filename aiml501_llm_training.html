LLM Training Infrastructure

The training process and resource costs of frontier generative AI models
Derrick Karake · AIML-501 Workshop 3 · Portfolio Artifact

The Training Pipeline — From Raw Data to Deployed Model

1

Data Collection

Crawl + curate hundreds of TB of text/code/images from the public web, books, code repos, and licensed corpora.

2

Cleaning & Filtering

Deduplicate, filter low-quality and harmful content, tokenize, and balance the mixture across languages and domains.

3

Pre-Training

Run weeks-to-months of distributed training on thousands of GPUs/TPUs using next-token prediction. This is the most expensive step.

4

Supervised Fine-Tuning

Train on human-written instruction–response demonstrations to teach the model how to follow instructions.

5

RLHF / Alignment

Learn from human preference rankings (and increasingly AI-generated feedback) to make responses safer, more useful, and more honest.

6

Evaluation

Run capability, safety, and red-team benchmarks. Iterate on the model until it meets release criteria.

7

Deployment

Serve via API or product. Inference runs on a fleet of GPUs/TPUs at far lower per-query cost than training but at much larger total scale.

The Four Cost Categories

📚

Data

Acquiring, licensing, cleaning, and labeling the training corpus. Includes scraping infrastructure, vendor data deals, and the human annotators who write demonstrations and rank responses.
Example: OpenAI is estimated to spend hundreds of millions of dollars per year on data alone — including licensing news, books, and code, plus thousands of contractors writing and rating responses.
🖥️

Compute

The dominant cost. Pre-training a frontier model requires tens of thousands of GPUs/TPUs running for weeks. Capital expense for the cluster, plus operating cost while it runs.
Example: GPT-4 is widely estimated to have cost between $50M and $100M+ in compute alone. Hardware procurement and depreciation push the total significantly higher.
⚡

Energy

Powering and cooling the cluster. Modern training runs draw tens of megawatts continuously, with substantial water consumption for cooling. Both an operational cost and an environmental footprint.
Example: A large training run can consume several gigawatt-hours of electricity — comparable to the annual energy use of a small town.
⏱️

Time

Pre-training runs span weeks to months. Failed runs or instabilities can erase weeks of progress. Time is a real cost because compute is rented or amortized while the run completes.
Example: Llama 3 (405B) was trained on a 16,000-GPU cluster over roughly 54 days. Any restart at scale is expensive.

Why This Matters

Frontier LLM training is now one of the most capital-intensive activities in software. The combination of data acquisition, compute, energy, and time means that only a handful of organizations in the world can afford to train models at this scale. That concentration has real consequences for who shapes AI capabilities and who benefits from them.

For engineers and product teams, the practical lesson is that you almost never need to train a frontier model from scratch. The cost-effective path is to start from a pre-trained open or licensed model and adapt it — through fine-tuning, retrieval, or prompt engineering — to a specific task. Understanding the cost stack above is what makes that build-vs-buy decision rational rather than aspirational.

For everyone, the energy and water footprint of training and inference is a reminder that AI is not a free, weightless utility. Responsible deployment means asking whether the value delivered by a model justifies its resource cost — at the model level, and at the per-query level.
